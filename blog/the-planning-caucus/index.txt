1b:T20c7,<p><em>January 19</em></p><figure><img alt="An open-source project lead runs a planning meeting with three robot advisors — design, product vision, and architecture — each contributing their perspective." src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*PNGmdE7LmymCRl8Ln_3v_A.png" /><figcaption>“OK, here’s the plan!”</figcaption></figure><p>One issue. Four meetings. A single subagent. 101 tests by end of day. That’s the shape of January 19th — the day we launched the MUX (modeled UX) track with what I’m now calling a “planning caucus”: a deliberate sequence of specialized consultations before any code was written.</p><h3>Implementing our objet model</h3><p>Issue #399: MUX Object Model Implementation isn’t a bug fix or a quick feature. This was foundational work — turning our conceptual object model (“Entities experience Moments in Places”) into actual running code. Eight months of thinking were about to become infrastructure.</p><p>The risk of getting it wrong was high. The cost of rework would be weeks, not hours. So instead of diving straight into implementation, I tried something different.</p><h3>The caucus</h3><p>I scheduled four planning sessions, each with a different specialist agent.</p><p>First the CXO tackled the first question: What does “experience” actually mean in our grammar? The design philosophy that emerged centered on three principles: awareness not data (entities perceive, they don’t just store), atmosphere not contents (places have character, not just items), and weight not sequence (some moments matter more than order). The key insight was that “experience” is the verb that needs the most attention — it’s what makes Piper Morgan different from a database with a chat interface.</p><p>Next the Principal PM addressed the second question: How do we translate design into implementation phases? The PPM took the CXO’s philosophy and turned it into actionable guidance. They added Phase 4.5 for canonical query tagging with lens mapping, revised the estimate from 20 to 31–32 hours, established success metrics at three tiers, and assigned Pattern-051 to the Lead Developer. The key insight: “Cathedral work, not velocity work — 40 hours done right beats 20 hours flattened.”</p><p>Then the Chief Architect provided technical direction. Use Protocols for role fluidity, not inheritance. Build lenses on the existing 8-dimensional spatial framework. Treat Situation as context manager, not data model. Cross-reference ADR-054 for persistence boundaries. The key insight: the infrastructure we need already partially exists — build on it, don’t rebuild it.</p><p>By midday, the Lead Developer had absorbed all the guidance and begun execution. An hour later, seven child issues were spec’d (#612-#618). On return two hours after that, Phase 0 investigation was complete. By 4:20 PM, a subagent was deployed for Phase 1.</p><h3>The subagent</h3><p>Here’s where it gets interesting.</p><p>The subagent deployed at 4:20 PM completed Phase 1 in 15 minutes. Not 15 hours. <strong><em>Fifteen minutes.</em></strong></p><p>Delivered: 15 source files (protocols, situations, perception models), 8 lens implementations, 17 test files, and 101 passing tests. All template requirements met.</p><p>How is that possible?</p><p>Because by the time the subagent started, every decision had already been made. The design philosophy was clear. The implementation pattern was specified. The success criteria were defined. The architectural constraints were documented.</p><p>The subagent’s job wasn’t to figure anything out. It was to execute a precisely specified plan.</p><p>This was possibly when I started realizing how far I’d come from vibe coding eight months ago.</p><h3>Why this worked</h3><p>I’ve run plenty of sessions where I go straight to the Lead Developer with “Here’s the issue, figure it out.” Sometimes that works. Often it doesn’t — there’s thrashing, rework, architectural drift, and the creeping suspicion that we’re building the wrong thing.</p><p>The planning caucus inverted that pattern. The old way goes implementation, then discovery, then correction, then more implementation. The caucus way goes discovery, then alignment, then specification, then implementation.</p><p>The four meetings took about 3 hours of total clocktime. The actual implementation (once we started) took 15 minutes for P1. Even accounting for P0 investigation time, the total time to working code was faster than “just start building” would have been.</p><p>More importantly, the confidence was higher. We weren’t hoping the implementation was right. We knew it was right because we’d validated the approach from three different angles before writing a single line of production code.</p><h3>The structure</h3><p>For future reference, here’s the caucus structure that worked. The CXO goes first with “What does this mean?” and outputs design principles. The PPM goes second with “How do we phase this?” and outputs implementation guidance. The Architect goes third with “What pattern do we use?” and outputs technical direction. The Lead Developer goes fourth to execute, outputting decomposition and deployment.</p><p>Each agent has a distinct lens. The CXO brings user experience and design philosophy. The PPM brings product priorities and success criteria. The Architect brings technical patterns and infrastructure. The Lead Developer brings execution and coordination.</p><p>Design philosophy informs product phases. Product phases inform technical patterns. Technical patterns inform execution. Reversing the order leads to solutions looking for problems.</p><p>The sequence helps but it’s really a facilitated discussion, not a linear shot. It can iterate or go back and forth. I’m mostly just the messenger although of course I put my spin on things and I am the source of all the original ideas.</p><h3>When to use it</h3><p>The planning caucus is probably overkill for bug fixes, small features, well-understood domains, and time-sensitive work.</p><p>It’s valuable for foundational infrastructure, work that will be hard to change later, domains where the team has different mental models, and anything where “getting it right” matters more than “getting it done.”</p><p>The MUX object model qualified on all four counts. We’ll be building on this foundation for months. A few hours of alignment now prevents weeks of rework later.</p><h3>The unspoken part</h3><p>I should admit something: I was prepared to facilitate back-and-forth. Disagreements between agents. Clarification requests. Maybe even a second round of consultations.</p><p>None of that happened.</p><p>Each agent took the previous memos seriously, built on them, and added their layer. The CXO didn’t contradict the existing object model work. The PPM didn’t override the design philosophy. The Architect didn’t ignore the product guidance.</p><p>I expected a bit more friction between the lenses, to be honest (and at times we do get disagreements that strengthen the results on resolution) but the fundamental design work we documented so well in December provided a smooth path forward once fully absorbed and applied.</p><p>I don’t know if that’s because the structured sequence forced coherence, or because we got lucky, or because the agents are better at synthesis than I expected. But the result was a planning session that felt less like a meeting and more like a relay race — each runner handing off the baton cleanly, the final runner crossing the finish line.</p><p><em>Next on Building Piper Morgan: The CLAUDE.md paradox, when we discovered that less can be more during work on January 22 and 23.</em></p><p><em>Have you ever tried sequencing specialist consultations before implementation? An hour later, seven child issues were spec’d (#612-#618). On return two hours after that, Phase 0 investigation was complete. By 4:20 PM, a subagent was deployed for Phase 1.</p><h3 name="6849" id="6849" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The subagent</h3><p name="a6ff" id="a6ff" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Here’s where it gets interesting.</p><p name="d705" id="d705" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The subagent deployed at 4:20 PM completed Phase 1 in 15 minutes. Not 15 hours. <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Fifteen minutes.</em></strong></p><p name="0175" id="0175" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Delivered: 15 source files (protocols, situations, perception models), 8 lens implementations, 17 test files, and 101 passing tests. All template requirements met.</p><p name="96a0" id="96a0" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">How is that possible?</p><p name="e0a2" id="e0a2" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Because by the time the subagent started, every decision had already been made. The design philosophy was clear. The implementation pattern was specified. The success criteria were defined. The architectural constraints were documented.</p><p name="3054" id="3054" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The subagent’s job wasn’t to figure anything out. It was to execute a precisely specified plan.</p><p name="dca8" id="dca8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This was possibly when I started realizing how far I’d come from vibe coding eight months ago.</p><h3 name="2021" id="2021" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Why this worked</h3><p name="98a7" id="98a7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">I’ve run plenty of sessions where I go straight to the Lead Developer with “Here’s the issue, figure it out.” Sometimes that works. Often it doesn’t — there’s thrashing, rework, architectural drift, and the creeping suspicion that we’re building the wrong thing.</p><p name="0713" id="0713" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The planning caucus inverted that pattern. The old way goes implementation, then discovery, then correction, then more implementation. The caucus way goes discovery, then alignment, then specification, then implementation.</p><p name="da9f" id="da9f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The four meetings took about 3 hours of total clocktime. The actual implementation (once we started) took 15 minutes for P1. Even accounting for P0 investigation time, the total time to working code was faster than “just start building” would have been.</p><p name="b929" id="b929" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">More importantly, the confidence was higher. We weren’t hoping the implementation was right. We knew it was right because we’d validated the approach from three different angles before writing a single line of production code.</p><h3 name="63d4" id="63d4" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The structure</h3><p name="d0d0" id="d0d0" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">For future reference, here’s the caucus structure that worked. The CXO goes first with “What does this mean?” and outputs design principles. The PPM goes second with “How do we phase this?” and outputs implementation guidance. The Architect goes third with “What pattern do we use?” and outputs technical direction. The Lead Developer goes fourth to execute, outputting decomposition and deployment.</p><p name="61b0" id="61b0" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Each agent has a distinct lens. The CXO brings user experience and design philosophy. The PPM brings product priorities and success criteria. The Architect brings technical patterns and infrastructure. The Lead Developer brings execution and coordination.</p><p name="fc53" id="fc53" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Design philosophy informs product phases. Product phases inform technical patterns. Technical patterns inform execution. Reversing the order leads to solutions looking for problems.</p><p name="1740" id="1740" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The sequence helps but it’s really a facilitated discussion, not a linear shot. It can iterate or go back and forth. I’m mostly just the messenger although of course I put my spin on things and I am the source of all the original ideas.</p><h3 name="0502" id="0502" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">When to use it</h3><p name="00db" id="00db" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The planning caucus is probably overkill for bug fixes, small features, well-understood domains, and time-sensitive work.</p><p name="8416" id="8416" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">It’s valuable for foundational infrastructure, work that will be hard to change later, domains where the team has different mental models, and anything where “getting it right” matters more than “getting it done.”</p><p name="08ad" id="08ad" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The MUX object model qualified on all four counts. We’ll be building on this foundation for months. A few hours of alignment now prevents weeks of rework later.</p><h3 name="28fc" id="28fc" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The unspoken part</h3><p name="824b" id="824b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">I should admit something: I was prepared to facilitate back-and-forth. Disagreements between agents. Clarification requests. Maybe even a second round of consultations.</p><p name="e0a5" id="e0a5" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">None of that happened.</p><p name="f48f" id="f48f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Each agent took the previous memos seriously, built on them, and added their layer. The CXO didn’t contradict the existing object model work. The PPM didn’t override the design philosophy. The Architect didn’t ignore the product guidance.</p><p name="7903" id="7903" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">I expected a bit more friction between the lenses, to be honest (and at times we do get disagreements that strengthen the results on resolution) but the fundamental design work we documented so well in December provided a smooth path forward once fully absorbed and applied.</p><p name="45fc" id="45fc" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p graf--trailing">I don’t know if that’s because the structured sequence forced coherence, or because we got lucky, or because the agents are better at synthesis than I expected.
