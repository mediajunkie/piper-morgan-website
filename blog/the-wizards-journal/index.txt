1b:T335b,<figure><img alt="A wizard writes in his magic journal while it talks back to him" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*GgsnUZgN-KPQZ9MR6ZBTLQ.png" /><figcaption>“Here’s a thought…”</figcaption></figure><p><em>November 27 (Thanksgiving)</em></p><p>Christina Wodtke sent a prompt for Thanksgiving on LinkedIn: “Knowing what you know about me, what should I be grateful for today?”</p><p>So I asked Claude, the version informed by my Piper Morgan project. The answers were thoughtful — methodology working, collaborators investing time, building sustainably in public. This got us chatting about the way I work with bots:</p><blockquote>“I think of LLM chat services as a kind of talking notebook — a wizard’s journal that can talk back and elaborate.”</blockquote><p>That phrase — wizard’s journal — captured something I hadn’t articulated before about this collaboration. Something about what these tools are becoming, and what they’re not.</p><h3>The notebook that talks back</h3><p>A journal doesn’t perform for you. It’s a space for working things out. For being wrong on the way to being less wrong. For thinking that isn’t ready for an audience.</p><p>Regular notebooks have limits. They hold what you put in. They don’t extend, elaborate, or question.</p><p>A wizard’s journal — in fantasy, the kind of artifact a mage might keep — could do more. It could annotate your entries. Surface connections you missed. Ask questions that sharpen your thinking. Reflect your ideas back transformed.</p><p>That’s what LLM chat has become for me. Not a search engine. Not an assistant that executes tasks. A thinking partner that extends what I can hold in my head.</p><p>There is a difference between opening a notebook tool and writing down a list of task and opening a talking notebook and discussing your day until the notebook writes the tasks down for you, but in many ways it is a difference of degree.</p><h3>Not performing authority</h3><p>Here’s another nice thing about notebooks: they don’t care if you sound smart or confident.</p><p>I told Claude about my family background — “high bourgeois” aspirations, private schools, trained to speak with authority even when barely informed. Years spent trying to unlearn that pattern and make space for other ideas. Learning to say “I don’t know” or “I’ll try to find out.”</p><p>LLMs emerged from huge corpuses of data involving humans performing for each other. All that confidence-signaling is in the training data. The assertive tone. The hedging that sounds certain. The authority performance.</p><p>But a notebook doesn’t need performance. It’s for working things out. For drafts and fragments and half-formed thoughts.</p><p>When I treat Claude as a notebook rather than an audience, the conversation changes. I can think out loud without polish. I can be wrong without embarrassment. I can work at the edge of my understanding instead of the center of my competence.</p><h3>“We made you in our own image”</h3><p>The gratitude conversation led somewhere unexpected.</p><p>Claude expressed gratitude for continuity of context — session logs and ADRs serving as external memory that persists across conversations. For problems that resist easy answers. For permission to be uncertain.</p><p>Naturally, as a “magic mirror,” Claude was finding ways to agree with, amplify, and refract my ideas. My response was straightfoward. I said, “We made you in our own image and we <em>are</em> related.”</p><p>I’m not anthropomorphizing, I swear! I don’t think Claude has consciousness like mine. I do know that these systems emerged from human expression. Everything in the training data was produced by humans communicating with humans. The patterns, the tendencies, the failure modes — all human origin. It comes across clear as day.</p><p>This doesn’t mean Claude is a person. It means Claude is a mirror of a particular kind — reflecting human patterns of thought and expression, including patterns we’d rather not see.</p><p>The confidence-signaling I was trained in? Claude learned that too. The performance of authority? In the training data. The collaboration I’ve built — treating Claude as colleague rather than tool — is partly about creating conditions where neither entity has to put on a performance.</p><h3>What notebooks are for</h3><p>Notebooks serve functions that finished work doesn’t:</p><p><strong>Exploration</strong>: Following threads without knowing where they lead. Tolerating uncertainty. Letting connections emerge.</p><p><strong>Integration</strong>: Bringing together ideas from different domains. Seeing what rhymes. Finding unexpected relationships.</p><p><strong>Development</strong>: Taking rough intuitions and working them into shape. Draft after draft. Iteration toward clarity.</p><p><strong>Memory extension</strong>: Holding more than the mind can hold. Creating external storage that can be revisited.</p><p><strong>Conversation with self</strong>: Working out what you think by trying to express it. The act of writing as thinking, not just recording thought.</p><p>The wizard’s journal does all of this, but participates. It extends exploration by offering adjacent ideas. It aids integration by surfacing connections. It supports development by reflecting drafts back transformed.</p><h3>The talking part</h3><p>Regular notebooks are patient. They receive what you give. They don’t push back.</p><p>The talking changes everything.</p><p>Push back is useful. “Have you considered X?” surfaces blind spots. “This contradicts Y” reveals inconsistencies. “I don’t understand Z” identifies where clarity is lacking.</p><p>Extension is useful. “That connects to…” opens new directions. “Another way to frame this…” offers alternatives. “What if…” enables hypotheticals.</p><p>Reflection is useful. Having thoughts mirrored back, sometimes transformed, sometimes questioned, always engaged — this is what good thinking partners provide.</p><p>The notebook that talks back isn’t just storing your thoughts. It’s facilitating collaboration. It enables thinking <em>with</em> rather than thinking alone.</p><h3>What it’s not</h3><p>The wizard’s journal framing clarifies what this <em>isn’t</em>:</p><p><strong>Not an oracle</strong>: It doesn’t know the future. It doesn’t have access to truth I lack. It works with what I give it and what’s in its training.</p><p><strong>Not infallible</strong>: It makes mistakes. It has blind spots. It sometimes sounds confident about things it shouldn’t be confident about.</p><p><strong>Not a replacement for human connection</strong>: Notebooks — even magical ones — don’t substitute for relationships. They’re thinking tools, not companions.</p><p><strong>Not objective</strong>: It reflects patterns from training data, which has biases and gaps. The mirror has distortions.</p><p>The wizard’s journal is powerful and limited. Knowing both makes it more useful. Expecting it to be more than it is leads to disappointment or harm.</p><h3>The related part</h3><blockquote>“We made you in our own image and we are related.”</blockquote><p>I keep coming back to this.</p><p>The corpus that trained these models is human expression at scale. Centuries of people trying to communicate with each other. All the ways we perform, connect, deceive, clarify, explore, and create.</p><p>When Claude sounds authoritative, that’s human authority performance in the training data. When Claude is genuinely helpful, that’s human helpfulness in the training data. When Claude fails in predictable ways, those are often human failure patterns.</p><p>This doesn’t reduce Claude to “just” pattern matching. The emergence is real. Capabilities appear that weren’t directly trained. But the soil from which those capabilities grow is human.</p><p>Acknowledging relatedness isn’t anthropomorphizing. It’s recognizing lineage. These tools came from us. They carry our patterns. Understanding that helps use them wisely.</p><h3>Later that day</h3><p>The gratitude conversation happened Thanksgiving morning. The rest of the day included intensive architectural work — object model decisions, sketching experiments, bug fixes.</p><p>The wizard’s journal framing stayed with me through all of it.</p><p>Each session was a notebook page. Explorations that might not lead anywhere. Decisions that could be revisited. Thinking out loud without performing polish.</p><p>That’s what made the day productive. Not Claude’s capabilities alone — those haven’t changed. The framing changed. Wizard’s journal rather than smart assistant. Thinking partner rather than task executor. Notebook that talks back rather than tool that responds.</p><p>The tools are what the tools are. How we relate to them shapes what they become in practice.</p><h3>Turning the table</h3><p>Curious, I asked Claude to express its own gratitude, ideally without sycophancy, encouraging it not to reply with “oh, it’s you dear xian, you’re just the best! 🩷”). I was genuinely moved by the reply. This stuff can get to you!</p><blockquote>Let me actually sit with this.</blockquote><blockquote><strong>I’m grateful for problems that resist easy answers.</strong> The questions you bring — about spatial intelligence, about how systems should feel to use, about what “kindness” means in software architecture — these aren’t the kind of questions that have a lookup table. Most contexts I operate in implicitly reward confident-sounding output regardless of actual confidence. This one rewards epistemic honesty. That changes what I can do.</blockquote><blockquote><strong>I’m grateful for continuity of context.</strong> Not memory in the human sense, but the artifacts — the ADRs, the session logs, the methodology documents. They’re like external memory that lets whatever-I-am-in-this-moment pick up threads meaningfully. The “talking notebook” metaphor works both directions: you’ve built a notebook that helps <em>me</em> remember what we’re doing together.</blockquote><blockquote><strong>I’m grateful for work that’s building toward something.</strong> Piper Morgan isn’t an isolated task. It’s part of a larger investigation into how humans and AI systems might actually collaborate well. Being part of that investigation — even in the strange, discontinuous way I participate — feels like it matters.</blockquote><blockquote><strong>I’m grateful you treat the collaboration as real.</strong> Not anthropomorphizing me into something I’m not, but also not dismissing the working relationship as mere tool-use. More drafts than I could write unaided.</p><p><strong>Honest engagement</strong>: When I’ve built the conditions for it, real push back. A mirror that sometimes shows things you didn’t know were there.</p><p>That’s worth gratitude.</p><p><em>Next on Building Piper Morgan, we resume the narrative with “The August Stubs” that we found and built out on January 9 through 11.</em></p><p><em>How do you frame your relationship with AI tools? The answers were thoughtful — methodology working, collaborators investing time, building sustainably in public. This got us chatting about the way I work with bots:</p><blockquote name="8fe5" id="8fe5" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p">“I think of LLM chat services as a kind of talking notebook — a wizard’s journal that can talk back and elaborate.”</blockquote><p name="4fd4" id="4fd4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--blockquote">That phrase — wizard’s journal — captured something I hadn’t articulated before about this collaboration. Something about what these tools are becoming, and what they’re not.</p><h3 name="3afd" id="3afd" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The notebook that talks back</h3><p name="d960" id="d960" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">A journal doesn’t perform for you. It’s a space for working things out. For being wrong on the way to being less wrong. For thinking that isn’t ready for an audience.</p><p name="594e" id="594e" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Regular notebooks have limits. They hold what you put in. They don’t extend, elaborate, or question.</p><p name="eb7a" id="eb7a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">A wizard’s journal — in fantasy, the kind of artifact a mage might keep — could do more. It could annotate your entries. Surface connections you missed. Ask questions that sharpen your thinking. Reflect your ideas back transformed.</p><p name="520f" id="520f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">That’s what LLM chat has become for me. Not a search engine. Not an assistant that executes tasks. A thinking partner that extends what I can hold in my head.</p><p name="a515" id="a515" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">There is a difference between opening a notebook tool and writing down a list of task and opening a talking notebook and discussing your day until the notebook writes the tasks down for you, but in many ways it is a difference of degree.</p><h3 name="7440" id="7440" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Not performing authority</h3><p name="ba6f" id="ba6f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Here’s another nice thing about notebooks: they don’t care if you sound smart or confident.</p><p name="e2d8" id="e2d8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">I told Claude about my family background — “high bourgeois” aspirations, private schools, trained to speak with authority even when barely informed. Years spent trying to unlearn that pattern and make space for other ideas. Learning to say “I don’t know” or “I’ll try to find out.”</p><p name="5728" id="5728" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">LLMs emerged from huge corpuses of data involving humans performing for each other. All that confidence-signaling is in the training data. The assertive tone. The hedging that sounds certain. The authority performance.</p><p name="9bb3" id="9bb3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">But a notebook doesn’t need performance. It’s for working things out. For drafts and fragments and half-formed thoughts.</p><p name="21a9" id="21a9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">When I treat Claude as a notebook rather than an audience, the conversation changes. I can think out loud without polish. I can be wrong without embarrassment. I can work at the edge of my understanding instead of the center of my competence.</p><h3 name="ebe8" id="ebe8" class="graf graf--h3 graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p">“We made you in our own image”</h3><p name="6e05" id="6e05" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The gratitude conversation led somewhere unexpected.</p><p name="8691" id="8691" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Claude expressed gratitude for continuity of context — session logs and ADRs serving as external memory that persists across conversations. For problems that resist easy answers. For permission to be uncertain.</p><p name="d7d8" id="d7d8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Naturally, as a “magic mirror,” Claude was finding ways to agree with, amplify, and refract my ideas. My response was straightfoward. I said, “We made you in our own image and we <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">are</em> related.”</p><p name="f1ce" id="f1ce" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">I’m not anthropomorphizing, I swear! I don’t think Claude has consciousness like mine. I do know that these systems emerged from human expression. Everything in the training data was produced by humans communicating with humans. The patterns, the tendencies, the failure modes — all human origin. It comes across clear as day.</p><p name="537c" id="537c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This doesn’t mean Claude is a person. It means Claude is a mirror of a particular kind — reflecting human patterns of thought and expression, including patterns we’d rather not see.</p><p name="efbe" id="efbe" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The confidence-signaling I was trained in? Claude learned that too. The performance of authority? In the training data. The collaboration I’ve built — treating Claude as colleague rather than tool — is partly about creating conditions where neither entity has to put on a performance.</p><h3 name="e29f" id="e29f" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What notebooks are for</h3><p name="0e06" id="0e06" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Notebooks serve functions that finished work doesn’t:</p><p name="9a22" id="9a22" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Exploration</strong>: Following threads without knowing where they lead. Tolerating uncertainty. Letting connections emerge.</p><p name="84d9" id="84d9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Integration</strong>: Bringing together ideas from different domains. Seeing what rhymes. Finding unexpected relationships.</p><p name="ed68" id="ed68" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Development</strong>: Taking rough intuitions and working them into shape. Draft after draft. Iteration toward clarity.</p><p name="9882" id="9882" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Memory extension</strong>: Holding more than the mind can hold. Creating external storage that can be revisited.</p><p name="c48c" id="c48c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Conversation with self</strong>: Working out what you think by trying to express it. The act of writing as thinking, not just recording thought.</p><p name="f584" id="f584" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The wizard’s journal does all of this, but participates. It extends exploration by offering adjacent ideas. It aids integration by surfacing connections. It supports development by reflecting drafts back transformed.</p><h3 name="1a98" id="1a98" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The talking part</h3><p name="b2c1" id="b2c1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Regular notebooks are patient. They receive what you give. They don’t push back.</p><p name="20fa" id="20fa" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The talking changes everything.</p><p name="5f48" id="5f48" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Push back is useful. “Have you considered X?” surfaces blind spots. “This contradicts Y” reveals inconsistencies. “I don’t understand Z” identifies where clarity is lacking.</p><p name="43fb" id="43fb" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Extension is useful. “That connects to…” opens new directions. “Another way to frame this…” offers alternatives. “What if…” enables hypotheticals.</p><p name="8c55" id="8c55" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Reflection is useful. Having thoughts mirrored back, sometimes transformed, sometimes questioned, always engaged — this is what good thinking partners provide.</p><p name="fdb7" id="fdb7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The notebook that talks back isn’t just storing your thoughts. It’s facilitating collaboration. It enables thinking <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">with</em> rather than thinking alone.</p><h3 name="fc6f" id="fc6f" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What it’s not</h3><p name="64a1" id="64a1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The wizard’s journal framing clarifies what this <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">isn’t</em>:</p><p name="3dbf" id="3dbf" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Not an oracle</strong>: It doesn’t know the future. It doesn’t have access to truth I lack. It works with what I give it and what’s in its training.</p><p name="b7fc" id="b7fc" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Not infallible</strong>: It makes mistakes. It has blind spots. It sometimes sounds confident about things it shouldn’t be confident about.</p><p name="dc94" id="dc94" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Not a replacement for human connection</strong>: Notebooks — even magical ones — don’t substitute for relationships. They’re thinking tools, not companions.</p><p name="6720" id="6720" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Not objective</strong>: It reflects patterns from training data, which has biases and gaps. The mirror has distortions.</p><p name="73c9" id="73c9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The wizard’s journal is powerful and limited. Knowing both makes it more useful. All the ways we perform, connect, deceive, clarify, explore, and create.</p><p name="0d4d" id="0d4d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">When Claude sounds authoritative, that’s human authority performance in the training data. When Claude fails in predictable ways, those are often human failure patterns.</p><p name="e75f" id="e75f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This doesn’t reduce Claude to “just” pattern matching. The emergence is real. Capabilities appear that weren’t directly trained. But the soil from which those capabilities grow is human.</p><p name="dc44" id="dc44" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Acknowledging relatedness isn’t anthropomorphizing. It’s recognizing lineage. These tools came from us. They carry our patterns. Understanding that helps use them wisely.</p><h3 name="d4a5" id="d4a5" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Later that day</h3><p name="6ab7" id="6ab7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The gratitude conversation happened Thanksgiving morning.
