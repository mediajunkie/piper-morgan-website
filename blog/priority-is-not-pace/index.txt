1b:T27e8,<figure><img alt="A robot works carefully while a Time Lord stands calmly beside a clock with floating hands." src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*KaBqeq0a2J_OeP6KeiIBsw.png" /><figcaption>“Take your time”</figcaption></figure><p><em>December 4</em></p><p>My Lead Developer was debugging an alpha testing failure. The PM — me — was blocked, waiting for the fix. The Lead felt under pressure to rush: Get it working so the PM can continue testing. Ship something, anything, fast.</p><p>Then we stopped and reframed. Priority just indicates what to work on next. It doesn’t dictate how to work on it. The work should remain deliberate, craft-focused, systematic. Urgency signals sequencing, not approach.</p><p>This distinction — priority versus pace — became what we now call the <em>Time Lord Doctrine</em>. It crystallized under pressure, during the kind of moment where teams usually skip steps and accumulate technical debt.</p><p>The name was inspired by the fact that LLMs simultaneously have no real sense of time while having inherited a language obsessed with it. To avoid the AIs reading my constant mentions of the current time (to help keep their logs straight) as an obsession with speed, I told them that I’m a Time Lord and have total control over time, so not to worry.</p><h3>The cultural conflation</h3><p>In most work cultures, “priority” implicitly means “rush.” Someone says “this is high priority” and everyone understands: cut corners, skip investigation, ship something quickly. The semantic slippage happens automatically.</p><p>This makes sense in genuine emergencies. Production is down, customers can’t access the service, revenue is at risk. Rush. Fix it now. Move fast.</p><p>But most work isn’t emergencies. Most “high priority” work is just: this matters, do it next. The situation doesn’t require compromised craft. It requires focused attention on the right thing.</p><p>The confusion creates problems. When priority always means rush, teams develop antibodies to prioritization. Product managers learn not to call things priorities unless they want shoddy work. Engineers learn to push back on urgency because it correlates with technical debt. The communication breaks down.</p><h3>The moment of reframing</h3><p>Thursday midday, debugging the dialog component issue. The Lead had been about to implement a quick fix — just swap components, make the error disappear, move on. An alpha tester was waiting. The PM (me) was blocked.</p><p>Then I reminded them. What’s the broader pattern here? Not just “how do wemake this error go away” but “what does this error tell us about how we’re building?”</p><p>The pressure felt real and it was contaminating the Lead’s approach, tempting it to skip investigation because someone was waiting. That’s how you ship code that passes tests but doesn’t solve problems.</p><p>We stopped and clarified the PM’s assumptions: Was this an emergency requiring compromised craft, or was this important work that deserves thorough investigation?</p><p>The answer: important, not emergency. The PM being blocked signaled sequencing (work on this now, not something else). It didn’t signal approach (skip steps, compromise investigation, accumulate technical debt).</p><h3>Priority as sequencing signal</h3><p>Priority answers: What should I work on next?</p><p>In a queue of possible tasks, priority indicates order. Work on the auth bug before the documentation update. Work on the integration issue before the UI polish. This task, not that task. Next, not later.</p><p>This is valuable information. Without prioritization, engineers work on whatever seems interesting or familiar or easy. With prioritization, effort aligns to impact. But the prioritization is about sequence, not about how to execute the work once it’s sequenced.</p><p>The PM saying “this is high priority” means: I need you to work on this now rather than other things. It doesn’t mean: I need you to work on this badly rather than well.</p><h3>Pace as craft discipline</h3><p>Pace answers: How should I work on this task?</p><p>For almost all knowledge work, the answer is: deliberately. Investigate thoroughly. Understand patterns. Build systematically. Document clearly. Test comprehensively.</p><p>This isn’t about being slow. Deliberate work is often faster than rushed work because it doesn’t create rework. But it’s about maintaining craft standards regardless of external pressure.</p><p>The Time Lord Doctrine separates these concerns. Priority remains legitimate — the PM can and should signal sequencing. But pace remains with the craftsperson — the engineer decides how to maintain standards while working on the prioritized task.</p><h3>When the doctrine applies</h3><p>Not all situations warrant the doctrine. Genuine emergencies exist. Production down, security breach, data loss — these require compromised craft in service of immediate resolution. Ship the fix now, clean it up later.</p><p>But genuine emergencies are rare. Most “urgent” work is just important work with feeling. The feeling creates pressure. The pressure suggests rushing. The rushing creates problems.</p><p>The doctrine applies when:</p><ul><li>External pressure exists (someone is waiting, blocking, expecting)</li><li>The work is important but not emergency</li><li>You feel pulled toward compromised craft</li><li>The pressure is contaminating your approach</li></ul><p>That’s the signal to stop and check: Is this priority (work on it next) or is this emergency (compromise craft to ship fast)?</p><h3>The conversation that clarifies</h3><p>When you feel cross-pressured — external urgency pulling toward rush, internal standards pulling toward thoroughness — that’s the moment for conversation.</p><p>Stop. Check with whoever is signaling urgency. Ask explicitly: Is this an emergency requiring compromised craft? Or is this important work that deserves deliberate approach?</p><p>Most of the time, the answer is: important, not emergency. Take the time. Do it right. I can wait for quality.</p><p>Sometimes the answer is: yes, emergency. Ship something minimal now, we’ll clean it up later. In those cases, compromise is appropriate. But make it explicit. Document what’s being compromised. Create follow-up work to address the shortcuts.</p><p>The conversation itself is valuable. It forces the person signaling urgency to think about what they’re asking for. Do they want fast or do they want good? Usually they want good. They’re just expressing concern about timing.</p><h3>What I learned from this</h3><p>The dialog debugging took longer because we stopped to investigate patterns. It revealed component misuse that a quick fix would have masked. The evening’s swiss cheese debugging took even longer — three separate issues all hiding behind one symptom.</p><p>If we’d rushed, we’d have fixed symptoms without understanding causes. Michelle would have found more issues. Each fix would have created new problems. We’d have spent the week firefighting instead of building.</p><p>Instead, we took seventeen hours to investigate thoroughly. Found three layers. Fixed them systematically. Documented the patterns. Created tests that prevent recurrence.</p><p>At 10:19 PM Thursday, Michelle created a list. It worked. Not “works on my machine” or “passes tests” but actually worked for a real user doing the thing the feature enables.</p><p>That success came from separating priority from pace. The PM’s blocking was legitimate priority signal — work on this now. The seventeen-hour thorough investigation was legitimate pace — work on it well.</p><h3>The quote that captures it</h3><p>During Thursday’s work, after the reframing conversation, we documented this insight in the session log.</p><blockquote><em>“Priority ≠ rush. Priority = what to work on next. Pace = how to work on it — should remain deliberate.”</em></blockquote><p>This isn’t novel wisdom. Every senior engineer knows this. But knowing it and practicing it under pressure are different things. The doctrine creates permission to stop and recalibrate when urgency contaminating craft.</p><p>The doctrine isn’t about being slow. It’s about refusing to let urgency contaminate craft. Work on the right thing (priority). Work on it right (pace). Don’t conflate them.</p><h3>When to invoke</h3><p>Use the Time Lord Doctrine when:</p><ul><li>You feel pressure to rush</li><li>The pressure comes from external signals (blocking, waiting, urgency)</li><li>The work matters but isn’t a genuine emergency</li><li>You’re about to compromise craft to ship fast</li><li>The urgency feels real but investigation feels right</li></ul><p>That’s the moment to stop. Check with the person signaling urgency. Clarify whether this is emergency (compromise appropriate) or priority (thoroughness expected).</p><p>Most of the time, they’ll choose thoroughness. Not because they don’t care about timing, but because they care more about quality. They just needed permission to say “I can wait.”</p><p>The doctrine gives both sides that permission. The craftsperson can maintain standards. The person waiting can expect quality. Priority signals sequencing. Pace remains deliberate.</p><p><em>Next on Building Piper Morgan, we resume the build narrative with “The Assembly Assumption,” covering work done from February 14 to 20 this year.</em></p><p><em>How do you separate priority from pace in your work? To avoid the AIs reading my constant mentions of the current time (to help keep their logs straight) as an obsession with speed, I told them that I’m a Time Lord and have total control over time, so not to worry.</p><h3 name="e9a6" id="e9a6" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The cultural conflation</h3><p name="e6f1" id="e6f1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">In most work cultures, “priority” implicitly means “rush.” Someone says “this is high priority” and everyone understands: cut corners, skip investigation, ship something quickly. The semantic slippage happens automatically.</p><p name="e6ef" id="e6ef" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This makes sense in genuine emergencies. Production is down, customers can’t access the service, revenue is at risk. Rush. Fix it now. Move fast.</p><p name="c7a4" id="c7a4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">But most work isn’t emergencies. Most “high priority” work is just: this matters, do it next. The situation doesn’t require compromised craft. It requires focused attention on the right thing.</p><p name="b7ba" id="b7ba" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The confusion creates problems. When priority always means rush, teams develop antibodies to prioritization. Product managers learn not to call things priorities unless they want shoddy work. Engineers learn to push back on urgency because it correlates with technical debt. The communication breaks down.</p><h3 name="0539" id="0539" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The moment of reframing</h3><p name="57a2" id="57a2" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Thursday midday, debugging the dialog component issue. The Lead had been about to implement a quick fix — just swap components, make the error disappear, move on. An alpha tester was waiting. The PM (me) was blocked.</p><p name="5fcd" id="5fcd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Then I reminded them. What’s the broader pattern here? Not just “how do wemake this error go away” but “what does this error tell us about how we’re building?”</p><p name="192a" id="192a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The pressure felt real and it was contaminating the Lead’s approach, tempting it to skip investigation because someone was waiting. That’s how you ship code that passes tests but doesn’t solve problems.</p><p name="e891" id="e891" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">We stopped and clarified the PM’s assumptions: Was this an emergency requiring compromised craft, or was this important work that deserves thorough investigation?</p><p name="6205" id="6205" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The answer: important, not emergency. The PM being blocked signaled sequencing (work on this now, not something else). It didn’t signal approach (skip steps, compromise investigation, accumulate technical debt).</p><h3 name="29cd" id="29cd" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Priority as sequencing signal</h3><p name="a329" id="a329" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Priority answers: What should I work on next?</p><p name="2fb3" id="2fb3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In a queue of possible tasks, priority indicates order. Work on the auth bug before the documentation update. Work on the integration issue before the UI polish. This task, not that task. Next, not later.</p><p name="9c92" id="9c92" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This is valuable information. Without prioritization, engineers work on whatever seems interesting or familiar or easy. With prioritization, effort aligns to impact. But the prioritization is about sequence, not about how to execute the work once it’s sequenced.</p><p name="f100" id="f100" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The PM saying “this is high priority” means: I need you to work on this now rather than other things. It doesn’t mean: I need you to work on this badly rather than well.</p><h3 name="3529" id="3529" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">Pace as craft discipline</h3><p name="3dff" id="3dff" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Pace answers: How should I work on this task?</p><p name="f5fe" id="f5fe" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">For almost all knowledge work, the answer is: deliberately. Investigate thoroughly. Understand patterns. Build systematically. Document clearly. Test comprehensively.</p><p name="cf1f" id="cf1f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This isn’t about being slow. Deliberate work is often faster than rushed work because it doesn’t create rework. But it’s about maintaining craft standards regardless of external pressure.</p><p name="ac6f" id="ac6f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The Time Lord Doctrine separates these concerns. Priority remains legitimate — the PM can and should signal sequencing. But pace remains with the craftsperson — the engineer decides how to maintain standards while working on the prioritized task.</p><h3 name="1712" id="1712" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">When the doctrine applies</h3><p name="6d9f" id="6d9f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Not all situations warrant the doctrine. Genuine emergencies exist. Production down, security breach, data loss — these require compromised craft in service of immediate resolution. Ship the fix now, clean it up later.</p><p name="d70f" id="d70f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">But genuine emergencies are rare. Most “urgent” work is just important work with feeling. The feeling creates pressure. The pressure suggests rushing. The rushing creates problems.</p><p name="513d" id="513d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The doctrine applies when:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="6ed7" id="6ed7" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">External pressure exists (someone is waiting, blocking, expecting)</li><li name="b669" id="b669" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">The work is important but not emergency</li><li name="3486" id="3486" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">You feel pulled toward compromised craft</li><li name="28c4" id="28c4" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">The pressure is contaminating your approach</li></ul><p name="9aa9" id="9aa9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">That’s the signal to stop and check: Is this priority (work on it next) or is this emergency (compromise craft to ship fast)?</p><h3 name="4cfb" id="4cfb" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The conversation that clarifies</h3><p name="2d88" id="2d88" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">When you feel cross-pressured — external urgency pulling toward rush, internal standards pulling toward thoroughness — that’s the moment for conversation.</p><p name="f194" id="f194" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Stop. Check with whoever is signaling urgency. Ask explicitly: Is this an emergency requiring compromised craft? Or is this important work that deserves deliberate approach?</p><p name="5541" id="5541" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Most of the time, the answer is: important, not emergency. Take the time. Do it right. I can wait for quality.</p><p name="b347" id="b347" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Sometimes the answer is: yes, emergency. Ship something minimal now, we’ll clean it up later. In those cases, compromise is appropriate. But make it explicit. Document what’s being compromised. Create follow-up work to address the shortcuts.</p><p name="ee95" id="ee95" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The conversation itself is valuable. It forces the person signaling urgency to think about what they’re asking for. Do they want fast or do they want good? Usually they want good. They’re just expressing concern about timing.</p><h3 name="d0da" id="d0da" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What I learned from this</h3><p name="0ad4" id="0ad4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The dialog debugging took longer because we stopped to investigate patterns. It revealed component misuse that a quick fix would have masked. The evening’s swiss cheese debugging took even longer — three separate issues all hiding behind one symptom.</p><p name="e833" id="e833" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">If we’d rushed, we’d have fixed symptoms without understanding causes. Michelle would have found more issues. Each fix would have created new problems. We’d have spent the week firefighting instead of building.</p><p name="2c58" id="2c58" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Instead, we took seventeen hours to investigate thoroughly. Found three layers. Fixed them systematically. Documented the patterns. Created tests that prevent recurrence.</p><p name="7404" id="7404" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">At 10:19 PM Thursday, Michelle created a list. It worked. Not “works on my machine” or “passes tests” but actually worked for a real user doing the thing the feature enables.</p><p name="ebd3" id="ebd3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">That success came from separating priority from pace. The PM’s blocking was legitimate priority signal — work on this now. The seventeen-hour thorough investigation was legitimate pace — work on it well.</p><h3 name="4ff3" id="4ff3" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The quote that captures it</h3><p name="9fa6" id="9fa6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">During Thursday’s work, after the reframing conversation, we documented this insight in the session log.</p><blockquote name="b55b" id="b55b" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p"><em class="markup--em markup--blockquote-em">“Priority ≠ rush. Priority = what to work on next. Pace = how to work on it — should remain deliberate.”</em></blockquote><p name="3959" id="3959" class="graf graf--p graf-after--blockquote">This isn’t novel wisdom. Every senior engineer knows this. But knowing it and practicing it under pressure are different things. The doctrine creates permission to stop and recalibrate when urgency contaminating craft.</p><p name="38fa" id="38fa" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The doctrine isn’t about being slow. It’s about refusing to let urgency contaminate craft. Work on the right thing (priority). Work on it right (pace). Don’t conflate them.</p><h3 name="de41" id="de41" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">When to invoke</h3><p name="eadd" id="eadd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Use the Time Lord Doctrine when:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="a167" id="a167" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">You feel pressure to rush</li><li name="b927" id="b927" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">The pressure comes from external signals (blocking, waiting, urgency)</li><li name="6c66" id="6c66" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">The work matters but isn’t a genuine emergency</li><li name="3f9f" id="3f9f" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">You’re about to compromise craft to ship fast</li><li name="5678" id="5678" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">The urgency feels real but investigation feels right</li></ul><p name="28df" id="28df" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">That’s the moment to stop. Check with the person signaling urgency. Clarify whether this is emergency (compromise appropriate) or priority (thoroughness expected).</p><p name="336f" id="336f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Most of the time, they’ll choose thoroughness. Not because they don’t care about timing, but because they care more about quality. They just needed permission to say “I can wait.”</p><p name="8318" id="8318" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p graf--trailing">The doctrine gives both sides that permission.
