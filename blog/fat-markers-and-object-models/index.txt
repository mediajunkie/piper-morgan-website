1b:T2a2f,<figure><img alt="A hand-drawn, black-and-white cartoon shows a human UX designer sketching simple, bold shapes on a whiteboard with a thick marker while a robot teammate looks on. Above the robot, a thought bubble contains a much more complex, interconnected diagram, suggesting a richer internal model formed from the rough sketches." src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*Lk_ClRYrB7Cyst9SON_a-Q.png" /><figcaption>“So, I kind of see it this way…”</figcaption></figure><p><em>November 27, 2025</em></p><p>I needed to understand an object model — substrates, lenses, lifecycles, metadata. The concepts had been discussed for two days. Decisions had been made. But I couldn’t see how they fit together.</p><p>So I grabbed fat markers and paper.</p><p>In parallel, I prompted three AI visualization tools with the same concepts. Same inputs. Different tools. Let’s see what each discovers.</p><p>The results surprised me. Not because AI tools failed — one produced genuinely elegant output. But because human sketching found things none of the tools could find.</p><p>Fat markers and AI tools discover different things. The combination is more powerful than either alone.</p><h3>The experiment</h3><p>Seven hand-drawn sketches on paper. Fat markers — the kind that force you to draw big, loose, imprecise.</p><p>Three AI tool outputs. Whimsical (diagramming), Eraser.io (technical drawing), and Gemini via Nano Banano (generative visualization).</p><p>Same concepts fed to each:</p><ul><li>Four substrates: Entities, Spaces, Moments, Situations</li><li>Eight perceptual lenses</li><li>Object lifecycle with composting stage</li><li>Metadata dimensions</li><li>How Piper understands users</li></ul><p>What would each approach reveal?</p><h3>What AI tools produced</h3><p><strong>Whimsical</strong>: Documentation, not insight. A text outline with connecting lines. Every concept represented as a labeled box. Relationships shown as arrows. Technically correct. Conceptually empty.</p><p>The diagram could have been generated from bullet points. It showed hierarchy but not meaning. Structure but not understanding.</p><p><strong>Eraser.io</strong>: Immediately jumped to database schema. The prompt said “object model” and the tool heard “data model.” Foreign keys. Table relationships. Implementation details.</p><p>This might be useful later, for engineering. But it lost all conceptual meaning. “Moment” became a table with columns, not a bounded scene where things happen.</p><p><strong>Gemini (Nano Banano)</strong>: Surprisingly effective. Overlapping colored regions showing how concepts interpenetrate. An elegant spiral for the lifecycle. Visual hierarchy that communicated importance.</p><p>This was the only AI output worth keeping. It captured gestalt — the overall shape and feel of the model. Not perfect (don’t look too close!) but directionally useful.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/0*wIhRLDhrYGJPXZjF" /></figure><h3>What hand-drawing discovered</h3><p>The seven sketches found things none of the AI tools found:</p><h4><strong>Substrates</strong></h4><p>Situation isn’t a fourth substrate parallel to Entity, Space, and Moment. It’s the <em>frame</em> — the container that holds a configuration of the other three. This changes the model fundamentally. A situation encompasses entities experiencing moments in places.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*x0mhnmpgOXRP5n3SKxDYmg.png" /></figure><h4><strong>Moments</strong></h4><p>The “shoebox” metaphor emerged. Moments are bounded containers — theatrical unities of time and place. Things happen inside them. They have beginnings and endings. The sketch led to “PPP” — Policy, Process, People (plus AI) as what shapes moments.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/437/1*p3iXZNKmcJ0oyuxLh5H2ng.png" /></figure><h4><strong>Lenses × entities</strong></h4><p>A triangle emerged showing change, flow, and causality as fundamental relationships. And the core grammar crystallized: “Entities experience Moments in Places.” This sentence captures the whole model.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*giSDaKWrmrB-3WsRea_-Kg.png" /></figure><h4><strong>Lifecycle</strong></h4><p>The shape of the cycle itself carries meaning. Circles imply repetition. Spirals imply progression. Arcs imply completion. The lifecycle diagram shouldn’t just list stages — its shape should communicate the kind of change.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*lRjkSLtJFZweHzFLYMo6xg.png" /></figure><h4><strong>What Piper knows</strong></h4><p>Drawing what Piper knows about an entity revealed that “Noticed” works better than “Inferred” as language for AI cognition. More human, less mechanical.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*7PKcsYnjQSs7v06GxoFzDg.png" /></figure><h4><strong>Human-centered</strong></h4><p>Bryce Glass’s Flickr-era pattern work came to mind. The principal sits in a situation. Piper delivers what they need. The sketch connected current work to design history.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*B-pjPddDCJR1Yd9xD6ESLQ.png" /></figure><h4><strong>Document handling</strong></h4><p>Piper as document intelligence hub.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/445/1*thH1bEvqy5teMUK2HLcAIg.png" /></figure><h3>Why fat markers matter</h3><p>Fat markers force generalization.</p><p>With a fine pen, you can add detail. You can be precise. You can make something look finished before you’ve figured out what it means.</p><p>With a fat marker, precision is impossible. You can’t draw small. You can’t add detail. You have to work at the level of shapes and relationships.</p><p>This constraint is productive. It prevents premature refinement. It keeps you working on structure when structure is what you need to understand.</p><p>The sketches weren’t pretty. They weren’t presentable. They were thinking tools — ugly and useful.</p><h3>What each approach is good for</h3><p>After the experiment, I could see what each approach offers:</p><p><strong>AI diagramming tools</strong> (Whimsical, Lucidchart, etc.): Good for documentation. Once you know what you’re representing, they produce clean, shareable diagrams. But they don’t help you figure out what you’re representing.</p><p><strong>AI code-oriented tools</strong> (Eraser.io, Mermaid): Good for technical translation. When you need to move from concepts to implementation, they help bridge the gap. But they assume you’ve already done the conceptual work.</p><p><strong>AI generative visualization</strong> (Gemini, Midjourney for diagrams): Good for gestalt. They can capture the overall feel, show how things interpenetrate, create visual metaphors. Worth including in the mix.</p><p><strong>Human sketching with fat markers</strong>: Good for discovery. Finding relationships you didn’t know existed. Challenging your assumptions. Generating new concepts.</p><p>The mistake is using one approach for everything. Documentation tools don’t help discovery. Discovery tools don’t produce documentation.</p><h3>The combination</h3><p>The best outcome combines approaches:</p><ol><li><strong>Discover</strong> with fat markers and paper. Find the relationships. Challenge the structure. Let unexpected insights emerge.</li><li><strong>Capture gestalt</strong> with generative AI. Get a visual that communicates the overall shape and feel.</li><li><strong>Document</strong> with diagramming tools. Once you know what you’re representing, produce clean shareable versions.</li><li><strong>Translate</strong> with technical tools. When it’s time to implement, bridge from concepts to code.</li></ol><p>Each tool for its strength. No tool for everything.</p><h3>What sketching teaches</h3><p>The sketching session taught me things about my own object model that two days of discussion hadn’t revealed.</p><p>Situation as frame, not peer substrate. That changes how I think about everything else. Entities don’t just exist alongside situations — they exist <em>within</em> situations.</p><p>“<strong>Entities</strong> experience <strong>Moments</strong> in <strong>Places</strong>.” That sentence emerged from a sketch, not from conversation. The act of drawing forced a synthesis that talking didn’t produce.</p><p>Lifecycle shapes as metadata. The spiral versus the circle versus the arc — these aren’t just aesthetic choices. They communicate different kinds of change. The sketch made that visible.</p><p>None of these insights were in the AI tool outputs. Not because AI can’t have insights — the Gemini visualization showed genuine understanding of gestalt. But because the insights required <em>me</em> to think, and the AI tools were thinking <em>for</em> me.</p><p>Sketching is thinking. Not recording thoughts — generating them.</p><h3>When to reach for markers</h3><p>The experiment suggests when fat markers help:</p><p><strong>Early conceptual work</strong>: When you don’t yet know what you’re building, sketch it. The constraints of fat markers keep you at the right level of abstraction.</p><p><strong>Stuck points</strong>: When discussion isn’t producing clarity, sketch it. The visual modality often unsticks verbal loops.</p><p><strong>Integration challenges</strong>: When you have many concepts that need to fit together, sketch their relationships. Paper is patient with uncertainty.</p><p><strong>Before using AI tools</strong>: Sketch first, then prompt AI tools. You’ll give better prompts because you’ll understand what you’re asking for.</p><p>The fat marker isn’t always the right tool, but it’s one of the best things to start with.</p><p><em>Next on Building Piper Morgan: “The Wizard’s Journal,” some thoughts on how I approach an LLM chat like a talking notebook, a diving pool, a magic mirror.</em></p><p><em>Have you discovered things through sketching that other tools missed? The concepts had been discussed for two days. Decisions had been made. But I couldn’t see how they fit together.</p><p name="5b9b" id="5b9b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So I grabbed fat markers and paper.</p><p name="1b53" id="1b53" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In parallel, I prompted three AI visualization tools with the same concepts. Same inputs. Different tools. Let’s see what each discovers.</p><p name="67c6" id="67c6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The results surprised me. Not because AI tools failed — one produced genuinely elegant output. But because human sketching found things none of the tools could find.</p><p name="1eb8" id="1eb8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Fat markers and AI tools discover different things. The combination is more powerful than either alone.</p><h3 name="9402" id="9402" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The experiment</h3><p name="23fb" id="23fb" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Seven hand-drawn sketches on paper. Fat markers — the kind that force you to draw big, loose, imprecise.</p><p name="b89d" id="b89d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Three AI tool outputs. Whimsical (diagramming), Eraser.io (technical drawing), and Gemini via Nano Banano (generative visualization).</p><p name="df5a" id="df5a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Same concepts fed to each:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="a756" id="a756" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">Four substrates: Entities, Spaces, Moments, Situations</li><li name="5600" id="5600" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Eight perceptual lenses</li><li name="e96a" id="e96a" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Object lifecycle with composting stage</li><li name="3a4b" id="3a4b" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Metadata dimensions</li><li name="02d3" id="02d3" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">How Piper understands users</li></ul><p name="6171" id="6171" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">What would each approach reveal?</p><h3 name="10a1" id="10a1" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What AI tools produced</h3><p name="5978" id="5978" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Whimsical</strong>: Documentation, not insight. A text outline with connecting lines. Every concept represented as a labeled box. Relationships shown as arrows. Technically correct. Conceptually empty.</p><p name="4d27" id="4d27" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The diagram could have been generated from bullet points. It showed hierarchy but not meaning. Structure but not understanding.</p><p name="7de1" id="7de1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Eraser.io</strong>: Immediately jumped to database schema. The prompt said “object model” and the tool heard “data model.” Foreign keys. Table relationships. Implementation details.</p><p name="ad5f" id="ad5f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This might be useful later, for engineering. But it lost all conceptual meaning. “Moment” became a table with columns, not a bounded scene where things happen.</p><p name="77d6" id="77d6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Gemini (Nano Banano)</strong>: Surprisingly effective. Overlapping colored regions showing how concepts interpenetrate. An elegant spiral for the lifecycle. Visual hierarchy that communicated importance.</p><p name="0d91" id="0d91" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This was the only AI output worth keeping. It captured gestalt — the overall shape and feel of the model. Not perfect (don’t look too close!) but directionally useful.</p><figure name="7292" id="7292" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*wIhRLDhrYGJPXZjF" data-width="1488" data-height="811" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*wIhRLDhrYGJPXZjF"></figure><h3 name="5ddb" id="5ddb" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--figure">What hand-drawing discovered</h3><p name="ee25" id="ee25" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The seven sketches found things none of the AI tools found:</p><h4 name="5c63" id="5c63" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Substrates</strong></h4><p name="23de" id="23de" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Situation isn’t a fourth substrate parallel to Entity, Space, and Moment. It’s the <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">frame</em> — the container that holds a configuration of the other three. This changes the model fundamentally. A situation encompasses entities experiencing moments in places.</p><figure name="7cad" id="7cad" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*x0mhnmpgOXRP5n3SKxDYmg.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*x0mhnmpgOXRP5n3SKxDYmg.png"></figure><h4 name="963f" id="963f" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Moments</strong></h4><p name="c233" id="c233" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">The “shoebox” metaphor emerged. Moments are bounded containers — theatrical unities of time and place. Things happen inside them. They have beginnings and endings. The sketch led to “PPP” — Policy, Process, People (plus AI) as what shapes moments.</p><figure name="7da2" id="7da2" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*p3iXZNKmcJ0oyuxLh5H2ng.png" data-width="437" data-height="326" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*p3iXZNKmcJ0oyuxLh5H2ng.png"></figure><h4 name="b4cb" id="b4cb" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Lenses × entities</strong></h4><p name="f0ef" id="f0ef" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">A triangle emerged showing change, flow, and causality as fundamental relationships. And the core grammar crystallized: “Entities experience Moments in Places.” This sentence captures the whole model.</p><figure name="a30f" id="a30f" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*giSDaKWrmrB-3WsRea_-Kg.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*giSDaKWrmrB-3WsRea_-Kg.png"></figure><h4 name="4907" id="4907" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Lifecycle</strong></h4><p name="47ca" id="47ca" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">The shape of the cycle itself carries meaning. Circles imply repetition. Spirals imply progression. Arcs imply completion. The lifecycle diagram shouldn’t just list stages — its shape should communicate the kind of change.</p><figure name="c1da" id="c1da" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*lRjkSLtJFZweHzFLYMo6xg.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*lRjkSLtJFZweHzFLYMo6xg.png"></figure><h4 name="fbca" id="fbca" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">What Piper knows</strong></h4><p name="79cd" id="79cd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Drawing what Piper knows about an entity revealed that “Noticed” works better than “Inferred” as language for AI cognition. More human, less mechanical.</p><figure name="3bc0" id="3bc0" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*7PKcsYnjQSs7v06GxoFzDg.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*7PKcsYnjQSs7v06GxoFzDg.png"></figure><h4 name="919a" id="919a" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Human-centered</strong></h4><p name="9a63" id="9a63" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Bryce Glass’s Flickr-era pattern work came to mind. The principal sits in a situation. Piper delivers what they need. The sketch connected current work to design history.</p><figure name="0770" id="0770" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*B-pjPddDCJR1Yd9xD6ESLQ.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*B-pjPddDCJR1Yd9xD6ESLQ.png"></figure><h4 name="59ed" id="59ed" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Document handling</strong></h4><p name="37dd" id="37dd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Piper as document intelligence hub.</p><figure name="39b2" id="39b2" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*thH1bEvqy5teMUK2HLcAIg.png" data-width="445" data-height="594" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*thH1bEvqy5teMUK2HLcAIg.png"></figure><h3 name="fadf" id="fadf" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--figure">Why fat markers matter</h3><p name="da3c" id="da3c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Fat markers force generalization.</p><p name="ec6f" id="ec6f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With a fine pen, you can add detail. You can be precise. You can make something look finished before you’ve figured out what it means.</p><p name="12e3" id="12e3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With a fat marker, precision is impossible. You can’t draw small. You can’t add detail. You have to work at the level of shapes and relationships.</p><p name="6245" id="6245" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This constraint is productive. It prevents premature refinement. It keeps you working on structure when structure is what you need to understand.</p><p name="17bb" id="17bb" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The sketches weren’t pretty. They weren’t presentable. Once you know what you’re representing, they produce clean, shareable diagrams. But they don’t help you figure out what you’re representing.</p><p name="1ca9" id="1ca9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">AI code-oriented tools</strong> (Eraser.io, Mermaid): Good for technical translation. When you need to move from concepts to implementation, they help bridge the gap. But they assume you’ve already done the conceptual work.</p><p name="77b4" id="77b4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">AI generative visualization</strong> (Gemini, Midjourney for diagrams): Good for gestalt. They can capture the overall feel, show how things interpenetrate, create visual metaphors. Worth including in the mix.</p><p name="17d4" id="17d4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Human sketching with fat markers</strong>: Good for discovery. Finding relationships you didn’t know existed. Challenging your assumptions. Generating new concepts.</p><p name="cd65" id="cd65" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The mistake is using one approach for everything. Documentation tools don’t help discovery. Discovery tools don’t produce documentation.</p><h3 name="ca6c" id="ca6c" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">The combination</h3><p name="225b" id="225b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The best outcome combines approaches:</p><ol class="postList"><li name="0785" id="0785" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">Discover</strong> with fat markers and paper. Find the relationships. Challenge the structure. Let unexpected insights emerge.</li><li name="ea2a" id="ea2a" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li"><strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">Capture gestalt</strong> with generative AI. Get a visual that communicates the overall shape and feel.</li><li name="1c49" id="1c49" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li"><strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">Document</strong> with diagramming tools. Once you know what you’re representing, produce clean shareable versions.</li><li name="7b82" id="7b82" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li"><strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">Translate</strong> with technical tools. When it’s time to implement, bridge from concepts to code.</li></ol><p name="920a" id="920a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">Each tool for its strength. No tool for everything.</p><h3 name="f589" id="f589" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What sketching teaches</h3><p name="70a3" id="70a3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The sketching session taught me things about my own object model that two days of discussion hadn’t revealed.</p><p name="6efe" id="6efe" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Situation as frame, not peer substrate. That changes how I think about everything else. Entities don’t just exist alongside situations — they exist <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">within</em> situations.</p><p name="d740" id="d740" class="graf graf--p graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p">“<strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Entities</strong> experience <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Moments</strong> in <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Places</strong>.” That sentence emerged from a sketch, not from conversation. The act of drawing forced a synthesis that talking didn’t produce.</p><p name="9271" id="9271" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Lifecycle shapes as metadata. The spiral versus the circle versus the arc — these aren’t just aesthetic choices. They communicate different kinds of change. The sketch made that visible.</p><p name="357f" id="357f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">None of these insights were in the AI tool outputs. Not because AI can’t have insights — the Gemini visualization showed genuine understanding of gestalt. But because the insights required <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">me</em> to think, and the AI tools were thinking <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">for</em> me.</p><p name="c908" id="c908" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Sketching is thinking. Not recording thoughts — generating them.</p><h3 name="151d" id="151d" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">When to reach for markers</h3><p name="f230" id="f230" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">The experiment suggests when fat markers help:</p><p name="d6dd" id="d6dd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Early conceptual work</strong>: When you don’t yet know what you’re building, sketch it. The constraints of fat markers keep you at the right level of abstraction.</p><p name="a37e" id="a37e" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Stuck points</strong>: When discussion isn’t producing clarity, sketch it. The visual modality often unsticks verbal loops.</p><p name="fbff" id="fbff" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Integration challenges</strong>: When you have many concepts that need to fit together, sketch their relationships.
