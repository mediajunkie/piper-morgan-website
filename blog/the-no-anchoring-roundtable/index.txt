1b:T198a,<p><em>March 14</em></p>
<p>I has this sudden instinct we'd been building the system backwards. I wanted to ask my agent team and get honest answers. This is not a small question. It's the kind of question where the first one to speak shapes how everyone else thinks about it.
</p>
<p>I made sure nobody spoke first.
</p>
<p>I sent the same question to four leadership roles — CXO, PPM, Architect, CIO — in parallel. Each one received the same context: a screenshot of Piper rejecting a reasonable PM query, the observation that a $0 ChatGPT wrapper would have handled it better, and the question: "Are we doing this backwards?"
</p>
<p>No shared channel. No group thread. No way for any role to see what the others were writing. The CIO had actually recommended this approach in an earlier session — independent parallel responses to prevent anchoring bias.
</p>
<h2>Four frames, one diagnosis</h2>
<p>The responses came back. Each role found a different way to describe the same underlying problem.
</p>
<p>The CXO described a bouncer at the door. The classifier was checking credentials and turning away anyone without the right ID. The eight contextual fallback messages we'd just shipped — carefully written, CXO-authored copy for when Piper couldn't help — were, in the CXO's own words, "a band-aid on exactly this wound."
</p>
<p>The PPM diagnosed a layer inversion. Piper used the LLM to <em>classify</em> queries but not to <em>respond</em> to them. Tokens spent deciding the system couldn't help, then not using the LLM to actually help.
</p>
<p>The Architect gave the most precise technical description: "We use the LLM to classify but not to respond." The routing architecture was structurally inverted — handlers were the default, the conversational floor was the last resort.
</p>
<p>The CIO reframed the model entirely: "The LLM is the floor, not the ceiling." Every query should reach the LLM with full project context. Structured handlers should make the response <em>better</em>, not <em>different</em>.
</p>
<p>Four independent analyses. Zero overlap in framing. Complete overlap in conclusion.
</p>
<p>This is why I maintain ongoing agent roles and personas. Each holds a different lens and I need the perspectives to see the full picture.
</p>
<h2>Why no-anchoring works</h2>
<p>Anchoring bias is one of the best-documented effects in decision science. The first number in a negotiation shapes the outcome. The first opinion in a meeting shapes the consensus. The first framing shapes how everyone thinks about the problem.
</p>
<p>In a traditional team meeting, the most senior person or the most confident speaker goes first. Everyone else adjusts — not because they're weak, but because they're human. Even devil's advocates anchor on what they're opposing.
</p>
<p>The no-anchoring approach eliminates this entirely. Each respondent works from the raw evidence, not from someone else's interpretation of the evidence. When they converge, the convergence is signal, not groupthink.
</p>
<p>When they <em>don't</em> converge, that's signal too — it means the problem is genuinely ambiguous and a simple vote or discussion would have produced false consensus.
</p>
<p>This is directly drawn from my experience with teams of, you know, people. I never tell a team what I already think about something before asking for ideas. I don't want to start everyone out inside the box.
</p>
<h2>The structural advantage</h2>
<p>Multi-agent teams have a natural advantage here that human teams don't: agents can't peek. When I send a question to four separate Claude sessions, there's no hallway conversation, no Slack thread, no body language in a conference room. The isolation is complete by default.
</p>
<p>Human teams can approximate this with written submissions before a meeting, or with anonymous polling. But the temptation to discuss before the formal response is always there. With agents, the protocol <em>is</em> the behavior. There's no gap between "we agreed to respond independently" and "we actually responded independently."
</p>
<p>The cost is that agents can't build on each other's thinking in real time. Our synthesis step — the PPM collecting all four memos and producing a unified document — partially compensates. But the real-time riffing, the "yes, and..." dynamic of a good meeting, is lost.
</p>
<p>For high-stakes diagnostic questions — "is our architecture wrong?" — the tradeoff favors independence. For creative brainstorming, it probably doesn't. The technique is a tool, not a universal method.
</p>
<p>The PPM took the lead on synethesizing the four memos, then the Architect reviewed the synthesis, then CXO and CIO approved. This four-step convergence pattern (independent → synthesize → review → ratify) seemed to work pretty smoothly!
</p>
<h2>When to use it</h2>
<p>Not every question deserves a no-anchoring roundtable. Most decisions aren't high-stakes enough, and the overhead — four independent responses plus synthesis — is real.
</p>
<p>But when you're asking a question where the wrong answer means months of wasted work, and where the natural social dynamics of your team might produce false consensus, it's worth the cost.
</p>
<p>The signals that suggest no-anchoring:
</p>
<p>The question is diagnostic, not creative. You're asking "what's wrong?" not "what should we build?" Diagnostic questions have a ground truth you're trying to find, not a possibility space you're trying to explore.
</p>
<p>The stakes justify the overhead. If the answer is going to redirect a sprint, change an architecture, or kill a feature, four independent perspectives are cheaper than one wrong consensus.
</p>
<p>You suspect anchoring is likely. If one person on the team has strong opinions and tends to speak first, or if the team has a recent experience that's likely to dominate their framing, isolation is protective.
</p>
<p>With my agent team, it's hard not to dominate. I'm the source of all of the original ideas, after all, whatever that means. Plus, I have the final say. I can always put my thumb on the scale. The last thing I need to do is stifle other perspectives right off the bat. Speaking last gets me the most comprehensive feedback.
</p>
