Why I Created an AI Chief of Staff

A flashback to early July, about five weeks into this project, when I realized I needed to establish another dedicated role on my team of LLMs.

July 3

Today I spun up a new type of AI conversation for Piper Morgan — not to write code or debug issues, but to help me manage the project itself. I call it my “Chief of Staff” and it might be one of my better architectural decisions. (My joke is that it’s my executive assistant but it prefers to be referred to as my chief of staff, so I indulge it.)

The problem: workstream proliferation

Building Piper Morgan started simply enough: write code, fix bugs, repeat. But seven distinct workstreams had emerged by this point:

  1. Core development (the actual building)
  2. Architectural integrity (constant vigilance against drift)
  3. Debugging and issue resolution
  4. Documentation maintenance
  5. Learning curation (lessons and patterns)
  6. Team updates at Kind Systems
  7. Public content (Medium series and LinkedIn)

Each workstream has its own context, priorities, and outputs. Worse, they’re interdependent — a debugging session yields architectural insights that need documentation updates and become blog content.

[Note: We’ve since evolved this structure — debugging eventually folded into development as it became less of a standalone concern, and we added “Running Piper Morgan” as its own workstream for user experience and operational insights.]

The cognitive-load crisis

My brain was becoming a messy context-switching machine. Mid-debugging, I’d remember I needed to update the team. While writing documentation, I’d spot an architectural pattern worth sharing. During blog writing, I’d realize I’d lost track of which decisions we’d made when.

The irony: I was building an AI assistant for Product Managers while struggling with my own PM challenges.

The solution: specialized context

Instead of trying to make every AI chat session handle everything, I created a dedicated “Chief of Staff” role with a specific focus:

  • Track decisions and risks across all workstreams
  • Maintain a living session log
  • Coordinate dependencies between streams
  • Remind me of what needs attention
  • Provide the “step back and look at the big picture” perspective

The implementation

The Chief of Staff chat has its own:

  • Session log artifact that persists across conversations
  • Clear scope — project management, not technical implementation
  • Memory of commitments — “remind me to do X when Y completes”
  • Cross-stream visibility — knows what each workstream needs

Early results

In just the first session, we:

  • Refactored my entire documentation system for better continuity
  • Created standardized templates for session handoffs
  • Established clear workstream priorities
  • Made a strategic decision about MCP integration
  • Queued documentation updates without losing debugging focus

The meta-learning

This approach recognizes that different types of thinking need different contexts. My debugging chat can go deep on technical details without worrying about documentation. My architecture chat can explore patterns without tracking task dependencies. And my Chief of Staff keeps everything coordinated.

It’s essentially applying separation of concerns to AI collaboration itself.

The broader implication

As AI becomes more integrated into complex work, we’ll need more sophisticated patterns than “one chat for everything.” Specialized AI contexts — each with their own focus, memory, and protocols — might be how we scale AI assistance beyond simple tasks.

Building Piper Morgan isn’t just teaching me about AI-assisted PM work. It’s teaching me how to work with AI assistants at scale.
