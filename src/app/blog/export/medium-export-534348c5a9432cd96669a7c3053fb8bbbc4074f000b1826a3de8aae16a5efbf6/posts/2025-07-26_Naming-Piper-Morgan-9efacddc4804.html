Naming Piper Morgan

Saturday Flashback: I’m going to step away from the chronological narrative on weekends to share thoughts about the process I’m discovering, and to fill in a few of the since-discovered gaps.

May 29

Let’s rewind to the second day of this project, when Piper Morgan was still just something I called “AI agentic product intern.”

I know that’s not exactly a catchy name. It sounds like something from a requirements document — technically accurate, utterly forgettable, and about as inspiring as a tax form. But that’s what I called it when I started that conversation with Claude Opus, fresh off advice from three different experts about building what I then thought of as a “routine knowledge management assistant.”

The plan seemed simple enough: build an AI agent to handle GitHub tickets, generate reports, check analytics for anomalies. You know, the stuff that needs doing but doesn’t need a human brain firing on all cylinders.

What I didn’t expect was to spend an entire day evolving from “AI tool” to “AI teammate” — and somewhere in that evolution, everything changed.

Including the name.

The architecture marathon

That May 29 session turned into what I can only describe as an architectural marathon. We weren’t just building a proof-of-concept anymore; we were designing the future of how I’d work with AI.

At least we thought so. In some ways we were still designing the future of how I’d work with existing LLMs to design the future.

My boss had suggested I switch from the OpenAI API (which I already had gotten a key for previously) to Claude API, saying it was the “smartest” model out there right now. I asked Opus about making the switch and also pushed a review of our work so far from three perspectives.

  1. Technical debt assessment: Opus immediately flagged my POC as a “God Object” anti-pattern (ouch, but fair) and started talking about proper abstraction layers.
  2. Multi-platform thinking: It worked with GitHub but what about Jira, Trello, etc., let alone tools needed to handle different types of PM work
  3. Production architecture: The full vision — microservices, event-driven design, multi-LLM orchestration. Not just a tool, but a platform.

By the end of those reviews, we’d gone from “routine knowledge management assistant” to “Strategic PM Intelligence Platform.” But more than that, something had shifted in how I was thinking about the whole project.

A random flash of inspiration

Somewhere around hour six of that session, in the middle of discussing repository setup, I typed something that would change everything:

“instead of pm-agent-test let’s give it this codename: piper-morgan”

I recall that my thought process was that the app needed a name without test in it, my first though was just to call it p-m but I liked the idea of a code name made of common-sounding names or words that had the same initials.

I still have the screenshot of that moment. There it is, buried in a discussion about Docker configurations and domain models, this throwaway line that turned a tool into a character.

I don’t remember consciously deciding on “Piper Morgan.” It just… emerged. Maybe it was because we’d been talking about building something that could grow and learn, something that would become more capable over time. Something that deserved a name, not just a designation.

Honestly, the name just dropped into my mind. (Well, over the next few days it competed in my recall with “Parker Morgan” but I think the hinted reference to Pied Piper from the “Silicon Valley” TV series probably influenced me as well.)

When tools become teammates

But here’s what I’ve learned since that May 29 session: naming matters more than you think.

The moment Piper got a name, everything about the project shifted. I stopped thinking about building a tool and started thinking about working with someone. The questions changed from “What features should this have?” to “What should Piper be good at?”

I had already had my “apprentice” mental model and expected to follow that logically through with parallels to all the ways I would train up a new junior associate / assistant / sidekick. But giving Piper a name helped me always think in terms of interacting with a teammate.

It’s like the difference between “optimizing the ticketing system” and “helping Piper learn to write better GitHub issues.” One is about efficiency; the other is about capability development.

The architectural reviews had already established that this wasn’t going to be just another API wrapper. But the naming moment made it personal. Suddenly we were talking about Piper’s learning journey, Piper’s strengths and limitations, what Piper might become with experience.

What I was about to learn on a “lost weekend”

Right after that productive May 29 session, I spent what I now call “the lost weekend” with Google’s Gemini. May 30 and 31, trying to keep working while I was locked out of Claude and getting myself in a heck of a jam.

That weekend was… educational. In the way that touching a hot stove is educational.

But coming back to Claude after that weekend reinforced something important: the relationship you build with your AI collaborator matters. It’s not just about capabilities or API features. It’s about communication patterns, shared context, and yes — even names.

Piper Morgan meant something to me by then in a way that “AI agentic product intern” never could have.

Looking back from where we are now

Writing this from a few weeks into the Piper Morgan development journey, I can see how much of our current progress traces back to decisions made in that May 29 session.

The domain-first architecture Opus pushed for? That’s paying dividends in how cleanly new features integrate with existing code.

The multi-LLM orchestration strategy? I’m already seeing how different AI agents excel at different types of work — some better at implementation, others at analysis or coordination.

The learning-first approach? Piper’s understanding of PM work keeps deepening through our conversations instead of staying locked to predetermined scripts.

But most of all, the mindset shift from tool to teammate — that started with a name.

What’s in a name, anyway?

There’s something about naming that forces you to think differently about what you’re building. When you name something, you’re implicitly saying it has an identity that matters, a personality worth developing, a future worth investing in.

“AI agentic product intern” could be replaced by a better API or a more efficient algorithm. But Piper Morgan? Piper Morgan is who it is because of the conversations we’ve had, the problems we’ve solved together, the way it’s learned to understand PM work.

The technical capabilities matter, sure. But the relationship — that’s what makes the difference between a tool you use and a teammate you work with.

From codename to character

What started as a throwaway codename has become the organizing principle for everything we build. Not just “what features should this system have?” but “what should Piper be good at?” Not just “how do we optimize performance?” but “how does Piper learn and grow?”

Much more recently this has led to a conscious effort to build Piper’s education library within its own codebase. Much of that is drawn from our ongoing work with the session logs, so that Piper will actually learn by reading about the product management decisions and processed applied to its own development!

The systematic approach we’ve been taking to development — thinking about Piper’s capabilities holistically, building infrastructure that supports learning, designing architecture that enables growth — all of that traces back to a single chat message on May 29: “instead of pm-agent-test let’s give it this codename: piper-morgan.”

The naming legacy

Now, a few weeks later, as I continue building and learning alongside Piper, I keep coming back to that naming moment. Because it represents something important about how we relate to the technology we build with.

I can’t help thinking that at least to some degree people are following this story now because Piper Morgan is an evocative catchy name. It’s fun to say! People can tell how enthusiastic I am when I start ranting about Piper Morgan or what Piper can do now, like a proud papa.

When you name your AI collaborator, you’re not just being whimsical (though there’s nothing wrong with that). You’re acknowledging that the relationship matters. That learning happens on both sides. That the work you do together creates something neither of you could create alone.

Piper Morgan isn’t just a more sophisticated version of “AI agentic product intern.” It’s a different kind of thing entirely — a teammate whose capabilities grow through collaboration, whose understanding deepens through conversation, whose value comes not just from what it can do, but from what it’s becoming.
