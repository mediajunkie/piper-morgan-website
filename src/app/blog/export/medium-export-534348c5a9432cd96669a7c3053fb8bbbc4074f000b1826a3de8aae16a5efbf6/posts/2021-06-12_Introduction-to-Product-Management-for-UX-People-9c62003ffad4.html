Introduction to Product Management for UX People

“Why is a product manager telling me what to do?”

Most user experience professionals remember the first time we found ourselves working with a product manager.

In a meeting.

For a new job.

At a bigger company.

In a different vertical.

At a startup.

For a reorg.

But however it happened, something was new… and who was this person in the meeting talking about “the product” and speaking for UX?!

“That’s the product manager, shhh.” One of the designers on your team says they’ll fill you in later. You see, it turns out UX reports to “product” here.

OK, but what does that mean? Who does what? Does this project — no product manager make the wireframes and then just hands them over to the designers to “color them in” and “make it pretty?”

And who has the final say on the product’s user experience?

Towards the end of this year Rosenfeld Media will be publishing my book Product Management for UX People: From Designing to Thriving in a Product World (you may sign up there to be notified when it is available for order), the culmination of a multiple-year project that has unfolded with the input and support of the Design in Product community.
During the editorial and production process I am sharing early drafts of chapters, and I welcome any feedback here that will strengthen the material.

What even is a product manager? These days, that depends on who by, where, and how the role is being executed.

Finally, what do UX practitioners need to know about “product.”

What we talk about when we talk about product:
Product is both a shorthand for the mouthful “product management” as well as a deliberate choice not to lean into that ambiguous mgmt part (it’s kind of like rock vs. rock ’n’ roll), and it also stands for product thinking in general, for product people, product design, product development, etc., taken as a whole.

Before we plunge in let’s run through the ritual liturgy. How do product and UX relate to each other, how do they work well together, what is the “correct” relationship between product and UX?

It depends.

OK, with that out of the way, from here on in I’ll cite about specific examples (thinly disguised if need be, or generalized if it’s a ubiquitous pattern) from so that you can plot your own mental model for where product fits into the UX world view (and vice versa).

Let’s start by rewinding a bunch of years…

Ten Years Ago I Took This Workshop…

A little over ten years ago I was a staff interaction designer at Yahoo and curator of the legendary design pattern library there (my business card read “Pattern Detective.” My boss at the time was a senior director of UED (user experience design) on the platform design team, Erin Malone, with whom I later co-authored a book on social experience design.

We were both presenting at the Information Architecture Summit that year and when we saw there was a workshop about product management for user experience designers (being taught by Jeff Lash and Chris Baum), we both jumped at the chance to sign up, I suspect for similar reasons.

You see, at Yahoo, UED reported into something called “the Product org.” All the technical work at the company (putting aside critical but less directly software-related roles in sales, HR, operations, and so on) was delivered in a collaboration between the Product org and the Engineering org. These two behemoths were locked in an endless battle for ascendancy that happened at all levels, up and down the line, across the company.

Our platform design team was actually in the Tech org, but even there UED had to work with and accommodate the demands of product management. (In fact, I was vetoed for the first job Erin recruited me to take on, on her team, by a product manager who worried I was “too senior” and wouldn’t be satisfied just cranking out mocks based on his wireframes.)

But more importantly, Yahoo as a whole treated user experience design (and research) as an arm of product. This was all new to me. I had cut my teeth first in the independent, art-oriented web of the 90s and then building websites for clients in freelance, agency, and consulting contexts. These sites were not product. They sold products, or advertised them, or promoted them. A big part of the appeal of Yahoo was getting closer to making functional experiences for people to engage with, and getting out the business of building microsites and revamping home pages and site navigation for various brick-and-mortar businesses.

So this ideas that were were making a product, and that product people governed our work, made a big impression on me, and Jeff and Chris’s workshop provided a great opportunity to learn more about the habits and drives of product people. It also left me with the seed of a new passion.

If You Can’t Beat ’Em…

Another moment that made a huge impression on me was the day Larry Cornett went from being the VP of User Experience Design for the Yahoo Search product to being the VP of Product for that same team.

“You can do that!?” I realized.

It was a few more years before I followed them over to what a lot of UX designers still to this day refer to as “the dark side.”

Did I become one of the oppressors? Do you have to switch teams to get ahead or to influence product strategy? Or can product and UX join forces as “super friends”? Stay tuned to find out more.
