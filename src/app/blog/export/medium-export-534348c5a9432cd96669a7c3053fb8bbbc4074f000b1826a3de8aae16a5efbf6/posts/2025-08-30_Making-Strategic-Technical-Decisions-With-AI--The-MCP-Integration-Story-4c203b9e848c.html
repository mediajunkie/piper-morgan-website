Making Strategic Technical Decisions With AI: The MCP Integration Story

This weekend, an architectural decision and a process improvement made with the advice of my then-new AI executive assistant (OK, chief of staff).

July 3

How do you evaluate emerging technology when you’re a solo developer with limited bandwidth? This decision to integrate MCP into Piper Morgan shows how AI can serve as a strategic technical advisor, not just a code generator.

The proposal arrives

My Principal Technical Architect chat (yes, I define specialized AI roles — I’ll own that) proposed integrating Model Context Protocol into Piper Morgan. MCP is Anthropic’s emerging standard for how AI systems connect to external tools — think USB for AI, if USB were still being designed.

The proposal was thorough: technical architecture, phased approach, risk assessment, resource requirements. But as a solo developer, I needed more than technical analysis. I needed to understand if this was the right move at the right time.

Remember, LLMs will tend to say yes if you ask if something is a good idea and are likely to recommend doing it now now now! if you don’t provide any other context or factors to consider.

Enter the Chief of Staff perspective

Rather than dive into implementation details, I handed the proposal to my Chief of Staff AI context. This shifted the conversation from “how to build it” to “when and why”:

  • How does this fit with current priorities?
  • What’s the opportunity cost of doing this now vs. later?
  • Where does it sequence with other work?
  • What are the cross-workstream implications?

The framing change was immediate and useful.

The strategic analysis

The Chief of Staff provided what it called a“workstream impact analysis”:

  1. Core build impact: Adds 5% more work but could accelerate the final 25%
  2. Architecture: Clean integration point, minimal drift risk from current patterns
  3. Documentation: Requires updates but creates valuable patterns for future work
  4. Learning content: Rich material for future posts (always a consideration)
  5. Public positioning: Shows forward-thinking architecture without being bleeding-edge

Then came the key insight: “MCP solves a real problem — tool proliferation — while maintaining architectural integrity.”

That framing helped me see this wasn’t just about adopting new technology. It was about positioning for inevitable complexity. I may be a magpie but I try to be selective about the shiny things I collect.

The decision framework

We landed on “prudent and bold” as the strategic approach:

Prudent first: Fix foundations

  • Complete workflow persistence fixes
  • Finish query implementation
  • Close current epic properly

Then bold: Leverage emerging standards

  • Implement MCP consumer support
  • Position for ecosystem growth
  • Build for tool proliferation rather than against it

The timeline decision

Rather than “someday maybe” or “drop everything now,” we created a specific trigger: Week 4+ after current debugging complete. This approach:

  • Respects current commitments (debugging always takes longer than expected)
  • Creates anticipation without pressure
  • Allows time for the standard to mature
  • Maintains momentum on current work

Documentation as decision artifact

Here’s where it gets methodical. The Chief of Staff created comprehensive handoff instructions for my documentation specialist context. This included ADR template, specific file updates needed, cross-referencing requirements, validation checklist.

By the time I was ready to implement, the decision rationale would be clearly documented and the work properly sequenced.

The meta-pattern for solo developers (and small teams relying on AI coding support)

This decision process reveals what’s working for me as a solo developer using AI assistance:

  1. Specialized contexts for different thinking modes (architect vs. strategist vs. implementer)
  2. Strategic layer separate from implementation details
  3. Documentation-first decision making that creates institutional memory
  4. Clear sequencing over fuzzy priorities
  5. Cross-context handoffs that preserve context between work sessions

The result in practice

In 30 minutes of structured conversation, I:

  • Evaluated an emerging technology against current priorities
  • Made a strategic timing decision with clear criteria
  • Sequenced it properly with existing work
  • Created implementation documentation for future me
  • Maintained focus on current debugging work

All without burdensome context-switching or losing track of what I was supposed to be doing that day.

What this suggests about AI collaboration

AI assistants aren’t just for writing code or fixing bugs. With proper context separation, they can serve as different members of a technical advisory team:

  • Principal Architect for evaluating technical proposals
  • Chief of Staff for strategic coordination and timing
  • Knowledge Management Specialist for capturing decisions and rationale
  • Implementation Assistant for actual execution

The key insight is recognizing that different types of decisions need different types of thinking — and structuring your AI collaboration to excel at each mode rather than expecting one conversation to handle everything.

This isn’t about replacing human judgment. It’s about having structured conversations that surface the considerations you need to make good decisions. The AI provides frameworks and analysis; you still make the calls.
