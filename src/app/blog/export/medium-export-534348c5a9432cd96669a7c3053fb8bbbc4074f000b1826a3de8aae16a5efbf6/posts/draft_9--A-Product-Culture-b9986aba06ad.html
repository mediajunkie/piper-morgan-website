9. Fostering a Product Culture

How do you create a culture that fosters and grows product people? Who are the product people you’ve already got (regardless of title)? Which of your talent can become product people? How do you hire and build a product team?

By this time I expect that you have a pretty good feel for what product mindset can do for your business. Cultivating such people so they thrive requires that you invest in a product culture that nurtures and empowers them. So how do you build a product organization, and where do you find these product people?

Building a product organization

Creating a real product organization starts with a commitment from the top, from you. Change is never easy and new habits cause friction when they clash with comforting older customs. Your executive championship of the product mindset will be a necessary, but not sufficient, ingredient in transforming your business and empowering your product people.

Next, you’re going to need to do the equivalent of a “build vs. buy” analysis that will depend a lot on your current situation.

  • Do you have a product org already, but is it what Marty Cagan calls a “feature factory” stuck in what Melissa Perri called “the build trap”? 
  • Do you ship software using agile methods with a lot of people called product owners or product managers but no coherent product strategy or roadmap?

If either of the above are true, then you’ll need to bring in new product leadership who will then empower the existing team or make staffing adjustments over time to enable this.

  • Do you build software currently without using a product model and without product roles on your teams?
  • Are you on the verge of making your first product hire?
  • Have you hired a head of product who has been unable to build a product practice and execute?

In these scenarios, you still have the opportunity to build a product org from scratch. The next step is to determine whether you’ve already got talent on staff prepared to take on product responsibilities, either by bringing a product mindset to their existing role or by shifting formally into a product management job.

Investing in your team

To begin with, you need to invest in product craft. Product people need to master these skills and techniques discussed throughout this book, and you need to help them do that. If you have senior product leadership in place (more on that in a moment) then it will be their responsibility to put in place the educational opportunities and events, the training programs, and to build an operation that provides career growth and professional development for product practitioners.

Product training should be offered to any practitioner who is interested, not just to people with product manager job titles. The goal is to nurture and validate product mindset in any role, across the entire organization.

If you do not yet have senior product leadership doing this, then the responsibility will fall to you until that role is filled. (It may make sense to work with a product consultant or a temporary head of product to jumpstart this process.)

A reliable way to broaden and strengthen product skills across your org is through cross-training. This depends on having at least one person who has mastered the skill that others are hoping to develop. Let’s take for example, tracking product analytical data, such as retention, and working systematically to improve it. 

This is an activity that involves a handful of techniques and skills that can be learned, among them:

  • capturing valid meaningful data
  • manipulating charts and graphs to analyze the data
  • engaging in discovery to understand what is going with customers that might explain the data
  • developing hypotheses about what might improve the experience
  • prioritizing experiments to test these hypotheses
  • learning from the outcomes of experiments and feeding this learning back into the discovery process

The best way to cross-train a product person who is more junior or who has less experience with these activities is to pair them with a senior product person who excels at these things. The key part is that the person who is learning does the work. The person who has the wisdom and experience provides coaching, guidance, and feedback. 

In the short term, these tasks will take a bit longer than if they were assigned to the experienced hand, but the benefit to distributing this expertise more widely on the team outweighs this cost, and the more cross-training goes on, the more supple and agile the product team will be, more able to flex, recombine, and adapt to handle changing circumstances.

Enabling transitions

When you are growing a product team out of the “soil” of your existing staff, you will want to identify people who show the sort of aptitude and instincts discussed throughout this book. They may be engineers, designers, marketers, business analysts, project managers, sales people, customer support staff, or really anything. 

Make it clear that you are building a product practice and that it will involve some hiring and that people in other roles will be involved as the entire enterprise takes on the habits of a product organization, but that you also welcome people formally transitioning into product management roles, and will facilitate this by providing training, coaching, and mentorship.

Hiring product people

When a business that has never had product managers before is on the verge of hiring their first product manager (or head of product), there can be a lot of confusion about where to start, what level of talent to bring on first, and how to start incorporating product management into existing processes.

There are two main variants of this junction in a company’s life:

  1. An established business that has been making and shipping software for years now and has not until now seen any need for or benefit from including product managers in the software development process.
  2. An early stage startup that consists primarily of one or two founders, a roomful of software engineers (possibly off-shore), and perhaps a handful of other operational roles.

The first scenario depends upon organizational change, and while it can be kindled at first with mid-level or junior product managers, the building of a truly empowered product organization won’t really get going without senior product leadership (either on staff or consulting).

For a startup, this can be an anxiety-filled junction, when the time comes to hire a first product person. The vast majority of my own consulting engagements these days are with startups that are literally trying to work out just exactly what product even is, when they need to hire their first product person, what level that person ought to be, and how to find them.

The truth is that in the early stages of a new startup, the founders need to “own” the product. I’d be somewhat suspicious of a founder who planned to outsource the core product work when getting a new business off the ground. But the time always comes when the CEO or operational founder has their hands full with too many things (hiring, dealing with investors or a board, sales, people operations, and so on) and need the leverage that only a dedicated product person can provide.

This is like a young parent getting a babysitter for the first time: terrifying. It only works if there is deep trust, understanding, and simpatico. A CEO who is lying awake worrying that their new head of product “doesn’t really get it” or might not be paying attention to the right things is getting less than no benefit from having delegated that work. What she needs is someone she fully believes does “get it,” is executing the agreed upon product strategy, and knows when to escalate tricky matters to the boss so that there are never any unpleasant surprises.

Product people are well aware of how challenging this first hire can be. Just the other day an entrepreneur I worked with years ago at Yahoo, Greg Cohn, joked to me that “everybody knows you want to be the second product hire.” And I knew just what he meant. There seem to be no end to businesses looking for an interim VP of Product to help from manage the transition period after the first candidate hired as a product head has washed out.

How senior the first product hire?

Another tricky aspect of the first product hire is determining the level of experience needed. In early stage startups it’s dangerous to make things too top-heavy, so it’s not uncommon to start with a junior or intermediate level product manager to help more with coordination and alignment and less with strategy and practice building. This can be a good way for the company to start learning product habits and sketching out operational processes that will be formalized over time as the teams evolve.

In more mature organizations, it’s critical to bring in senior product leadership. Titles vary, but tend to be one of the following

  • head of product — typically used by companies trying to keep things ambiguous and flexible in terms of level
  • director or senior director of product — tends to be a middle manager
  • VP (or SVP) of product — should have P&L responsibility
  • Chief product officer — represents the product org as a peer of other major business functions

The meanings and levels of these titles vary widely from company to company, and tend to map to equivalent HR levels for similar titles in other practices. Putting the specific titles that make the most sense in your organization to one side, the key question is where the buck stops and who speaks for product at the highest levels.

So, for example, you’re going to have a chief product officer. You may not hire anyone with that title, but if you don’t then the most senior product person will not be in the C suite and will report to someone who is. This may be the CEO, the COO, the CTO, or even some other role (less likely), but in any of those cases that person is de facto the chief product officer and will have the responsibility of championing the product culture.

If you do have a CPO, then that person is of course also the head of product and its redundant to give someone else that title (unless they are head of product “for” some subset of the product portfolio, say). But if the chief product office has other responsibilities besides product, then you will need a head of product (or VP or director) who will be the highest ranking person on the product staff and work closely with the CEO (or whomever they report to) to drive product strategy and operations.

Product expert (and a mentor of mine) Rich Mironov makes the point that when you are hiring your first head of product they should have experience managing product teams. This may sound dead obvious, but you’d be surprised. As he notes in his article Hiring a Head of Product he notes that the three most common errors he’s encountered in decades of consulting are:

  1.  Exec teams don’t know what Heads of Product do, or want someone who will “make development more efficient.”
  2. They don’t value experience running product management teams. Instead, they overweight narrow technical or market segment familiarity
  3. Each department wants to hire in its own image. Sales execs want more sales-y Heads of Product; development execs want more engineering-ish Heads of Product; marketing teams want a marketer in product clothing… no candidate passes muster with all groups.

Leadership teams

A successful first product lead can make progress in building a product team, and growing product people, but as you scale, it will become clear relatively quickly that there are several aspects of leadership that need to be handled, and over time no one single person can field them all. (It’s not so much that no one person is capable of addressing all these aspects. It’s more that there are only so many hours in a day, too much context switching is inefficient, and people need to focus!)

In a recent blog post on maturing design leadership teams, Peter Merholz (a design executive and consultant) made a similar point about design teams (which as we know are closely adjacent to product), parsing the various leadership roles into these categories:

  1. Executive leadership responsible for vision, championship, standards, recruiting (such as a CPO or VP Product).
  2. Operations leadership establishing workflow processes, program management, training and resources (Product Ops).
  3. Directors who manage major groupings of the team as whole, maintain quality, hire for their teams, manage careers (Director of Product).
  4. Principals or high-level individual contributors who demonstrate and spread craft leadership (Group Product Managers, Principal Product Managers).

Filling all these types of roles on day one would be overkill, but as a product org scales, all these aspects of leadership become critical and require many hands to fulfill.

Start with one strong lead, and give them the mandate to build a product culture.

Key Takeaways

  • Whether building a product org from scratch or transforming a feature factory into an empowered product culture, you may be able to convert some existing staff into product people and you will likely need to hire some new talent as well.
  • An ongoing commitment to training people in product craft will help build a product mindset across your org.
  • Recruit product managers from other roles.
  • Determine how senior your product leadership needs to be at first and who will be championing product management in the C suite.
  • The first head of product you hire is always a difficult choice, and the risk of failure for anyone attempting to carve out a product practice where none yet exists is always high.
  • Consider working with a consultant or interim head of product if the perfect leader doesn’t present themselves right when you need them.
  • Over time you will need to cultivate multiple types of product leaders.
  • You can do this!
